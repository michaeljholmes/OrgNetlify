# Show-and-Tell Process

## 1. Purpose

The Show-and-Tell (or Demo) is a regular, informal meeting where the engineering team demonstrates recently completed work to a wider audience. The primary goals are:

-   **Transparency**: To provide stakeholders with visibility into the progress of development.
-   **Feedback**: To gather early feedback on new features and functionality from a non-technical perspective.
-   **Alignment**: To ensure the work being delivered meets business and user expectations.
-   **Celebration**: To recognize and celebrate the team's accomplishments.

## 2. Cadence & Schedule

-   **Frequency**: Bi-weekly (every two weeks).
-   **Duration**: 30-45 minutes.
-   **Scheduling**: A recurring calendar invite will be sent to all participants.

## 3. Participants

-   **Presenters**: Engineering team members who have completed work they wish to demonstrate.
-   **Audience (Required)**: Product Owner, BA, UI/UX Designers, Engineering Team.
-   **Audience (Optional)**: Leadership, other department stakeholders (e.g., Marketing, Sales, Support).

## 4. Format & Agenda

The session is facilitated by the Product Owner or a Team Lead.

1.  **Introduction (2 mins)**: The facilitator briefly welcomes everyone and outlines the agenda.
2.  **Demos (25-40 mins)**:
    -   Engineers present their work.
    -   Each demo should be concise (5-7 minutes).
    -   Focus on demonstrating the *user value* and the working software, not the underlying code.
    -   Presenters should briefly state the problem they solved and then walk through the solution.
3.  **Q&A and Feedback (Throughout)**:
    -   The audience is encouraged to ask questions and provide constructive feedback after each demo.
    -   This is not a formal sign-off; it's a feedback session. Any required changes or bugs found are logged as new work items in the backlog.
4.  **Wrap-up (1 min)**: The facilitator thanks everyone for their time and participation.

## 5. What to Demo

-   Completed features that have reached the 'Ready for UAT' or 'Done' stage.
-   Significant UI/UX improvements.
-   Backend changes that enable new user-facing capabilities (even if the UI is not fully complete, the value can be explained).
-   Prototypes or proof-of-concepts for upcoming, complex features.
